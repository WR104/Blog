Apophenia/Patternicity

Zhenyu Jia
2022-07-10
philosophy
thinking, turkey, writing

what is apophenia?

In your daily life, you may have seen a human face in a coffee cup, or a cloud that looks like a rabbit, or a moon with a sad expression. In fact, this phenomenon is called apophenia or patternicity, and it happens every day of our lives. The brain is like a pattern detector that associates what you see with what you are familiar with. Psychologists call this phenomenon apophenia. That is, people tend to find connections between unrelated things. There is nothing wrong with these connections in themselves, but there is nothing special about them. Importantly, the connections people see reveal more of the brain’s mysteries than descriptions of what the brain associates.

For example, the famous Rorschach inkblot test uses graphic pattern delusions to try to understand what conclusions the patient will draw in the moment. Of course, these tests are difficult to interpret, because once you start thinking, you will be associated with a variety of things for a variety of reasons. The idea that a cloud in the sky hides a pattern is also an example of apophenia, which occurs frequently in everyday life because “it part and parcel of what it is to be human.”[1]

Humans are complex and contradictory creatures that

We are meaning seekers creatures and we are predisposed to find meaningful patterns in all sorts of events, even if those events are random in nature.[1]

This is the result of human intellect. And all mature minds have the impulse or tendency to be forced into this problem. Life is a journey to find meaning and answers, at most of the time they are at a loss. We are afraid of randomness and chaos, and our reason tells us that there must be a rational explanation for everything. Through our genes, we hope to control everything possible. Therefore, our instinctive reaction is to fight against randomness and turn them into something meaningful. We see an elephant in the cloud because we are “scared” to see only ordinary clouds.

Russell’s Turkey

Russell’s Turkey is one of my favorite theories that explain the idea of apophenia.

It states that there was a flock of turkeys on a farm, and the farmer came to feed them at eleven o’clock every day. A scientist among the turkeys noticed this phenomenon and kept observing it for almost a year without exception, it turns out that he discovered a great law: “Every day at eleven o’clock in the morning, food comes.” He announced this law to the turkeys on Thanksgiving morning, but on that day at eleven o’clock in the morning food did not come, and the farmer came in and caught them and killed them all.[2]

This story reveals that people instinctively believe in things that happen over and over again, and are unrealistically optimistic about the connections behind them. As the most intelligent species on the planet, “We’re bad, as human beings, at understanding coincidences”.[3] It is noticed that one is not only trying to find non-existent bonds in the randomness of disorder, but also in the order of repetition. People seek order in chaos out of fear, and in order to find meaning out of conceit.

see significance and meaning in random and chaotic events

Throughout human history, natural phenomena have been frequently associated with gods and religions. For instance, The pandemic has led “many theists to believe that the coronavirus is a punishment from Almighty God.”[3]

In fact, anyone with a deep understanding of history knows that this notion of sickness and suffering as a punishment has been common since the dawn of civilization. Therefore, Christopher Hitchens, a great skeptic, reminds us that “we remain dutifully unaware of how probability is at the heart of many natural disasters”.[3] Humans have tried to connect their understanding of an all-powerful and loving God with the endless suffering that surrounds them since “humanity is so good at pattern seeking (and seeing meaning in things that are merely random)”.[3]

Zodiac sings? Seriously?

What amazes me more is people’s craze for horoscopes. The fact that astrology and fortune telling have endured shows how much we desire understanding. This desire far outweighs our suspicions, and we do not question how accurate these understandings are. The horoscope is undoubtedly untrustworthy when viewed with the innate rational intuition of human beings. Just a date of birth cannot change a life’s origin, upbringing, life encounters, and impose a certain series of personality traits, developmental fortunes on an individual. Are all the countless individuals born in a month destined to have some similar traits in a random genetic sequence just because their parents created the little life 10 months ago? With human’s innate rational intuition, horoscopes are undoubtedly not credible. It is not acceptable to human reason that a date of birth can impose a certain set of character traits and developmental fortune on an individual, regardless of his or her origin, upbringing, and life circumstances. In summary, some people will believe in horoscopes, of which there are psychological and communication reasons. Materialists use reason to understand the world and get to the truth by reason, which does not mean to take down all the idealists. The act of imposing one’s worldview on these sensual minds is disgusting.

Humans are born with a need for faith and fantasy, it’s how they survive and live romantically.

From a philosophical standpoint, yes, I believe trying to find meaning in random events helps us comfort ourselves. For example, if a friend dies, it would be better to think that there is some reason behind the tragedy. People look for rationality in chaos - that is, for patterns, for meaning - because it is evolutionarily unfavorable to anything that is not understood as chaos. Some people believe that the terrible reaction to universal chaos is actually an extension of the same force that drove the evolution of the brain. In fact, it promotes the maturation of the brain in everyone’s life.
